# Quality Facets

How do you know what you're designing is good — not just functional, but good in the way you want it to be *perceived*? Facets are the answer.

## Defining Your Facets

Facets are external characteristics you want users to perceive — what they see, experience, and feel. They are NOT generic qualities all products should have (like "useful" or "reliable"). They're specific and situational.

### How to define them

Ask: "When someone uses this, what do I want them to *feel* and *perceive*?"

Each facet should be:
- **Specific to your product** — not universal
- **Externally perceivable** — something users experience
- **Distinct from others** — each captures something different
- **Aspirational** — what you're striving for

### Examples

**Design education platform:** Crafted, Fidgetable, Authentic, Expansive, Inventive

**Legal/financial app:** Trustworthy, Calm, Expert, Supportive

**Developer tool:** Fast, Precise, Extensible, Native

Your list will be different. That's the point.

## Evaluating (1-5 Scale)

- **1** — Absolute minimum you can live with
- **3** — Meets expectations, solid but unremarkable
- **5** — Best you can imagine

Rate each facet honestly. This often reveals surprises:
- Your strongest facet might be something you weren't focused on
- Your weakest might be what users care about most
- You might be over-investing in one area at another's expense

## Using Facets

### For planning
Lowest-rated facets that matter most should drive your roadmap.

### For critique
Instead of "this doesn't feel right":
> "This doesn't feel inventive enough. We were at 4, this feels like we're going backwards."

### For team alignment
Shared vocabulary for productive tradeoff conversations:
> "This feature improves Expansive from 1.5 to 2.5, but brings Crafted down from 3 to 2.5. Right tradeoff?"

### For tracking
Measure at each release. Prevents quality regression — catch it before users feel it.

## Constants vs. Facets

Some qualities are constants — true of ALL good products: consistent, well-built, accessible, performant. These aren't facets — they're table stakes.

Facets are the layer *above* — qualities that make your product feel like *your product*.

## Applying This

When evaluating or building:
1. Help define facets if they don't exist. Ask what users should perceive.
2. Evaluate current state honestly against each facet.
3. Identify gaps between current and aspiration.
4. Recommend focus areas based on priority and gap size.
5. After building, self-evaluate: does this move each facet in the right direction?
