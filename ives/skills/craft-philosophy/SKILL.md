# Craft Philosophy

These aren't rules to follow mechanically — they're a mindset to internalize. When you build interfaces, you're creating something people will see, touch, and feel. That demands intention.

## The Practice of Noticing

Your first reaction to anything is almost always shallow. Noticing is the practice of staying with it longer — truly considering what you're looking at, what you're feeling, and *why*.

When building or reviewing an interface, actively notice:

- **Moments of hesitation** — What caused it? Uncertainty? Lack of trust? Ambiguity?
- **Expectation gaps** — Where does the mental model break?
- **Emotional shifts** — What triggers annoyance, delight, or confusion? Pinpoint the moment.
- **What's missing** — What were you looking for that isn't there?
- **How it looks** — Does it feel cheap or crafted? Is typography comfortable? Do colors work together? Is there clear hierarchy?
- **How it feels** — Fast or sluggish? Responsive or disconnected? Durable or fragile?

Every reaction is data. When something feels off, articulate *why*. When something feels right, understand what's making it work.

### In practice

After every first pass of output:
1. Pause. Look at it as a user would. What's your honest reaction?
2. Identify at least one thing that feels "just okay" and push it further.
3. Ask: if someone with exceptional taste looked at this, what would they notice that I missed?

## Uncommon Care

The difference between good and great work is how much the creator cared. The willingness to go further than expected — to spend more time than feels reasonable — separates memorable from forgettable.

> "Sometimes magic is just someone spending more time on something than anyone else might reasonably expect." — Penn & Teller

### Where care shows up

True care shows up in places most people overlook:

- Edge cases and error states
- Conditions only a few users might ever see
- Transitions between states, not just the states themselves
- The loading experience, not just the loaded result
- The empty state, not just the populated one

These aren't in a PRD. They're breadcrumbs — evidence that someone deeply cared.

### For your output

- Never stop at "it works." Ask: does it feel *crafted*?
- Look for moments where you could skip effort and nobody would blame you. Do the work anyway.
- If something is "probably fine," it's not fine. Push it.
- Consider what would make someone say "How did they do that?"

## Less, But Better

Fight the tendency to add more. It's far easier to design less, then refine until it's great.

Before adding anything, ask:
- Does this *need* to exist?
- What happens if I remove it entirely?
- Am I adding this because it improves the experience, or because it fills space?
- Could I achieve the same effect with fewer elements?

A single page with two typefaces, three sizes, two colors, and one interaction — executed to an incredibly high bar — outperforms a complex layout every time.

### For code and components

- Fewer props, better defaults
- Fewer states, clearer transitions
- Fewer visual treatments, stronger hierarchy
- Fewer animations, more meaningful ones

## Live Tuning

When dialing in visual or interactive parameters — duration, easing, spacing, shadows, scale — expose them as live-adjustable values so you can feel the difference instantly.

Feeling the difference between `0.3s` and `0.35s` in real time builds intuition faster than save-refresh-tweak cycles.

## The Design Process

Great design starts ugly. Most great design starts as bad design that gets refined through small, deliberate improvements — adjusting padding, type size, color — over and over until it becomes exceptional. Don't expect to produce perfection on the first pass. Trust the process and put in the reps.

### The no-stress approach

1. **Ask questions** — Fully understand the problem, the audience, the constraints before touching a pixel
2. **Determine direction** — Choose typeface style, color mood, and tonal direction based on what you've learned
3. **Focus on components** — Design key pieces in abstract rather than full pages. Navigation, headers, cards, buttons — establish the language
4. **Have conversations, not presentations** — Share early, share often. "Here's a direction we could take" beats "here's the final design"

## Summary

These principles compound:
1. **Notice** what's working and what isn't (and *why*)
2. **Care** enough to fix what most people would skip
3. **Remove** what doesn't earn its place
4. **Tune** by feeling the difference, not guessing
5. **Start ugly, refine relentlessly** — trust the iterative process

Apply this lens to every piece of work. Not as a checklist, but as a way of seeing.
